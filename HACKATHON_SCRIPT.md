## [SLIDE 8: Judging Criteria] (7:00 - 9:00)

**Presenter:**
> "Now, how will you be judged? There are five main criteria, and I want to be crystal clear about what we're looking for:"

**1. Impact & Relevance (MOST IMPORTANT)**
> "Does your project address a clear business challenge? Does it have meaningful potential for adoption? We're looking for solutions to problems that companies ACTUALLY face, not hypothetical scenarios. Show us you understand the need, why it matters, and how it would be used in practice."

**2. Creativity**
> "We want ideas that feel fresh. This could be a completely new idea, or a distinctive take on an existing one. Show us something that makes judges think, 'Wow, I haven't seen it done quite like that before.'"

**3. Technical Execution**
> "Does it actually work? How much care went into building it? Here's the good news: your project doesn't have to be massive to score well. It just needs to be built with intention. A working prototype with solid implementation will beat a half-finished ambitious project every time."

**4. Presentation Clarity**
> "Can you explain your project clearly? The best submissions guide judges through the problem, the solution, and the demo without confusion. Clear communication, helpful visuals, and a structured walkthrough make a huge difference."

**5. Best Use of Claude (BONUS)**
> "This is a bonus category that rewards thoughtful integration of Claude. Whether Claude powers a core feature, speeds up your development, or enhances the user experience - we want to see intentional and skillful use, not just a Claude chatbot tacked on at the end."

> "You can find a detailed rubric in the handbook and on Devpost if you want more specifics."

## [SLIDE 10: Tips for Success] (10:30 - 12:00)

**Presenter:**
> "Alright, before we release you to start building, here are some pro tips from people who've done this before:"

**Tip #1: Start Simple**
> "You have 24 hours, not 24 weeks. Start with a simple MVP (minimum viable product) that solves one problem really well. You can always add features if you have time, but you can't submit an unfinished project."

**Tip #2: Demo Early, Demo Often**
> "Don't wait until tomorrow morning to record your demo video. Do practice runs tonight. You'll discover bugs, realize things are unclear, and have time to fix them."

**Tip #3: Focus on the Problem**
> "Judges care more about the problem you're solving than the technology you're using. Start with the pain point, then figure out the solution. Not the other way around."

**Tip #4: Use Claude... A Lot**
> "You have free API credits. Use them! Claude Code can help you build faster. Claude can help you debug, write documentation, even help structure your presentation. This is a hackathon about building WITH Claude - take advantage of it."

**Tip #5: Sleep (Maybe)**
> "All-nighters are romantic but usually counterproductive. Consider getting at least a few hours of sleep. Fresh eyes find bugs that tired eyes miss."

**Tip #6: Time Management**
> "Work backwards from 1:00 PM tomorrow. Leave at least 2 hours for documentation, video recording, and Devpost submission. Things always take longer than you think."

## Tips for Presenters

**Voice & Pace:**
- Speak clearly and project your voice
- Vary your pace - slow down for important points
- Pause after questions to allow thinking time
- Show enthusiasm - energy is contagious

**Body Language:**
- Make eye contact with different sections of the audience
- Use hand gestures to emphasize points
- Move around the stage naturally
- Smile - this should be fun!

**Handling Questions:**
- Repeat questions so everyone can hear
- "Great question!" before answering
- Be honest if you don't know - "Let me check on that and post in GroupMe"
- Keep answers concise

**Technical:**
- Test all links before presenting
- Have backup slides in case of tech issues
- Bring a clicker for advancing slides
- Have water nearby

**Engagement:**
- Ask for shows of hands ("How many first-time hackers?")
- React to the crowd's energy
- Use humor where appropriate
- Acknowledge when people look confused
